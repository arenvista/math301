# Chapter 2 - The Real Numbers

## 2.1 The Algebraic and Order Properties of $\mathbb{R}$
A *binary operation* on a set $S$ is a map $B: S \times S \rightarrow S$ (e.g., $+$, $-$, $\cdot$, $\exp$, etc.).

### [The Algebraic Properties of $\mathbb{R}$](1766959914-XULY.md) 
On the set $\mathbb{R}$, there are two binary operations, $+$ and $\cdot$, which satisfy the following axioms:

#### Addition 
* **Commutative Property of Addition**
    * $\forall a,b \in \mathbb{R}, \quad a+b = b+a$ 
* **Associative Property of Addition**
    * $\forall a,b,c \in \mathbb{R}, \quad (a+b)+c = a+(b+c)$ 
* **Existence of Zero / Additive Identity**
    * $\exists ~0 \in \mathbb{R}$ s.t. $\forall ~a \in \mathbb{R}, \quad 0+a=a \land a+0=a$
* **Existence of Additive Inverse**
    * $\forall ~a \in \mathbb{R}, \exists ~(-a) \in \mathbb{R}$ s.t. $a+(-a)=0 \land (-a)+a=0$

#### Multiplication
* **Commutativity of Multiplication**
    * $\forall ~a,b \in \mathbb{R}, \quad ab=ba$
* **Associativity of Multiplication**
    * $\forall ~a,b,c \in \mathbb{R}, \quad (ab)c = a(bc)$
* **Existence of Unit Element / Multiplicative Identity**
    * $\exists ~1 \in \mathbb{R}$ ***distinct from zero*** s.t. $1 \cdot a=a \land a \cdot 1=a$
* **Existence of Multiplicative Inverses**
    * $\forall ~a \in \mathbb{R}, a \neq 0, \exists ~(\frac{1}{a}) \in \mathbb{R}$ s.t. $a \cdot \frac{1}{a} = 1 \land \frac{1}{a} \cdot a=1$
* **Distributive Properties of Multiplication Over Addition**
    * $a(b+c) = (ab) + (ac)$

*From these axioms, we can prove the rest of algebra.*

---

## [[1766961657-ZUWT|2.1.3 Theorem - Zero Product Property and Multiplicative Inverses]]
* **(a) Uniqueness of Mult. Inverses**: If $a \neq 0$ and $b \in \mathbb{R}$ are such that $ab=1$, then $b=\frac{1}{a}$.
* **(b) Zero Product Property**: If $a \cdot b = 0$, then either $a=0 \lor b=0$.

**Proof of (b):**
> *Suppose $ab=0$ and $a \neq 0$. Goal: Show $b=0$.*
> 
> 1. Invoke Multiplicative Identity:
>    $$b = 1 \cdot b = b$$
> 2. Existence of Multiplicative Inverse (since $a \neq 0$):
>    $$1 \cdot b = (\frac{1}{a} \cdot {a})b$$
> 3. Associative Property of Multiplication:
>    $$(\frac{1}{a} \cdot {a})b = \frac{1}{a} \cdot (ab)$$
> 4. By Supposition ($ab=0$):
>    $$b = \frac{1}{a} \cdot (ab) = \frac{1}{a} \cdot (0) = 0$$

## [Building Sets in $\mathbb{R}$](1766962739-AMFZ)

### Natural Numbers
We have $0, 1 \in \mathbb{R}$.
* We can build a copy of $\mathbb{N} \in \mathbb{R}$ by assigning to each $n \in \mathbb{N}$ the number $1+1+1+...+1$ ($n$-times).

### Integers
To get the rest of $\mathbb{Z}$, use $-n = \text{inverse of } n$.
$$
\begin{array}{ccccccccccccccc}
\cdots & \longleftarrow & -2 & \longrightarrow & -1 & \longrightarrow & 0 & \longrightarrow & 1 & \longrightarrow & 2 & \longrightarrow & \cdots \\
\end{array}
$$

### Rational Numbers
Build $\mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{R}$ by taking each $q\in \mathbb{Q}$ to be $\frac{a}{b}$ for some $a,b \in \mathbb{Z}, b \neq 0$ so that $q = a \cdot \frac{1}{b}$.
* **Subfields**: Since $\mathbb{Q}$ is a field and $\mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{R}$, we say $\mathbb{Q}$ is a ***subfield*** of $\mathbb{R}$.

### Irrational Numbers
The set $\mathbb{R}\backslash\mathbb{Q}$ (or $\mathbb{Q}'$) contains the irrational numbers.

## [2.1.4 Theorem - Irrationality of $\sqrt{2}$](1766962818-EODE)

**Statement:** $\not \exists ~r \in \mathbb{Q} \ni r^2=2$.

### Proof
> Suppose for the sake of contradiction:
> * Let $p,q \in \mathbb{Z}, q \neq 0$.
> * $\exists ~r \ni r=\frac{p}{q}$.
> * Assume $\frac{p}{q}$ are in their **lowest terms** (implies $p,q$ have no common divisors greater than 1).
> 
> By supposition ($r^2=2$):
> $$
> \begin{align*}
> (\frac{p}{q})^2 &= 2 \\
> \frac{p^2}{q^2} &= 2 \\
> p^2 &= 2q^2
> \end{align*}
> $$
> 
> **Observation:**
> * $2q^2$ is even $\implies p^2$ is even.
> * $p^2$ is even $\implies p$ is even ($p=2k, k \in \mathbb{Z}$).
> 
> Substitute $p=2k$:
> $$
> \begin{align*}
> (2k)^2 &= 2q^2 \\
> 4k^2 &= 2q^2 \\
> 2k^2 &= q^2
> \end{align*}
> $$
> * This implies $q^2$ is even, so $q$ is even.
> 
> **Contradiction:**
> This demonstrates that $p,q$ are both divisible by two. However, we assumed $p,q$ were in their lowest terms.
> $$\implies \not \exists ~p,q \ni r \in {\mathbb{Q}} \implies \sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q}$$

## [2.1.5 Order Properties of $\mathbb{R}$](1766962878-RPBP)

There is a nonempty subset $\mathbb{P}$ of $\mathbb{R}$, called the set of **positive real numbers**, with the following properties: 

1. **$\mathbb{P}$ Closed under Addition**
    * $\forall ~a,b \in \mathbb{P}, (a+b) \in \mathbb{P}$
2. **$\mathbb{P}$ Closed under Multiplication**
    * $\forall ~a,b, \in \mathbb{P}, a \cdot b \in \mathbb{P}$
3. **Trichotomy Property**
    * $\forall ~a \in \mathbb{R},$ exactly one of the following holds:
        * $a \in \mathbb{P}$ (Positive)
        * $(-a) \in \mathbb{P}$ (Negative)
        * $a = 0$ (Zero)

* If $a \in \mathbb{P}$, we write $a>0$ (**strictly positive**).
* If $-a \in \mathbb{P}$, we write $a<0$ (**strictly negative**).
* If $a \in \mathbb{P} \cup \{0\}$ we write $a \geq 0$ (**non-negative**).

### [2.1.b Theorem - Trichotomy on Positive Real Numbers](1766963059-MASZ)
Let $a,b \in \mathbb{R}$. The Trichotomy Property implies:
$$
\begin{align*}
a-b \in \mathbb{P} &\implies (a>b) \\
-(a-b) \in \mathbb{P} &\implies (b>a) \\
a-b = 0 &\implies (a=b)
\end{align*}
$$
Also:
$$a-b \in \mathbb{P}\cup \{0\} \implies (a \geq b)$$

## [Ordering on $\mathbb{R}$](1766969917-MJUI.md)
An **ordered set** is any set $S$ on which $\forall ~a,b \in S$, either $a<b, a>b, a=b$.

### 2.1.7 Theorem - Rules of Inequality
Let $a,b,c \in \mathbb{R}$. The following statements hold:
* **Transitivity:** $(a>b) \land (b>c) \implies a>c$
* **Addition:** $(a>b) \implies (a+c) > (b+c)$ 
* **Multiplication (Positive):** $(a>b) \land (c>0) \implies ca > cb$
* **Multiplication (Negative):** $(a>b) \land (c<0) \implies ca < cb$

### 2.1.8 Theorem

**(a)** If $a \in \mathbb{R}$, $a \neq 0$, then $a^2 > 0$

> **PF:** If $a \neq 0$ then either $a \in \mathbb{P}$ or $(-a) \in \mathbb{P}$
> $\mathbb{P}$ is closed under mult/m.
> $a^2 = a \cdot a$ and $a^2 = (-a)(-a)$. //

**(b)** $1 > 0$.

> **PF:** $1^2 = 1 \cdot 1 > 0$ but $1^2 = 1 > 0$ //

**(c)** If $n \in \mathbb{N}$, then $n > 0$.

> **PF:** Induction.
> **Base case:** $1 > 0$.
> **Inductive step:** Suppose $k > 0$. Then $k+1 = \text{sum of two pos. \#s}$

# 2.2 [Absolute Values and The Real Line](1766974687-YHMG.md)

## 2.2.1 Absolute Value Defn.
The absolute value of $a \in \mathbb{R}$, denoted $|a|$, is given by:

$$
|a| = 
\begin{cases}
a, a>0 \\
0, a=0 \\
-a, a<0 
\end{cases}
$$

## 2.2.2 Theorem
Consider these values as members of $\mathbb{R}$; The following statments will hold:
* $|ab| = |a||b|$
* $|a|^2 = a^2$
* $c \geq 0 \implies |a| \leq c \iff -c \leq a \leq c$
* $-|a| \leq a \leq |a|$

Abs. values can be used to talk about the *distance* between objects in $\mathbb{R}$

# [Neighborhoods](1767053195-BYCC.md)
In order to talk about distance between objects we use neigborhoods.

## Defn.

Let $a \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\epsilon > 0$. Then the $\epsilon$-neigborhood of $a$ is the set:

$$
\begin{align}
V_{\epsilon}(a): 
& = \{x \in \mathbb{R} : |x-a| < \epsilon \} \\
& = \{x \in \mathbb{R} : -\epsilon < x-a < \epsilon \} \\ 
& = \{x \in \mathbb{R} : a-\epsilon < x < \epsilon+a \} \\ 
& = (a-\epsilon, a+\epsilon)
\end{align}
$$

Note $(a-\epsilon, a+\epsilon)$ is the set of points that are $\epsilon$-close to $a$

## Theorem

$$
\begin{align}
\text{Let } a \in \mathbb{R}\\ 
x \in V_{\epsilon}(a) \land \forall ~\epsilon, \epsilon > 0 \\
\implies x = a
\end{align}
$$

**Proof**
$$
\begin{align}
\text{Suppose: }& x \in V_{\epsilon}(a) \land \forall ~\epsilon, \epsilon > 0 \\
& \implies \forall ~\epsilon, |x-a| < \epsilon \\
& \implies |x-a| = 0 \\
& \implies x-a = 0 \\
& \implies x = a \\
& \end{align}
$$



# [Triangle Inequality](1766975367-NRVZ.md)

> Statement: $a,b \in \mathbb{R} \implies |a+b| \leq |a| + |b|$

**Proof**
$$
\begin{align}
-|a| & \leq a \leq |a| \\
-|b| & \leq b \leq |b| \\
-|a|-|b| & \leq a+b \leq |a| + |b| \\
-(|a| + |b|) & \leq a+b \leq |a| + |b| \\
-c & \leq a+b \leq c \\
& \implies |a+b| \leq c \\
& \implies |a+b| \leq  |a| + |b| \\
\end{align}
$$

_Why the name?_: 
* Similar statement for length of vectors

$$ ||\vec{a} + \vec{b}|| \leq ||\vec{a}|| + ||\vec{b}|| $$

> ![triange_inequality.png](assets/imgs/triange_inequality.png)

## Common Technique

Maybe we know something about a third point $y$

$$
\begin{align}
\text{Let } x,a,y \in \mathbb{R} & ~|x-a| \\ 
& = |x-y+y-a| \\ 
& = |(x-y)+(y-a)| \leq |x-y| + |y-a| \\ 
\end{align}
$$

## Corollary: 
$$
\begin{align}
& a_1, a_2, a_3,...,a_n \in \mathbb{R} \\
& \implies |a_1 + a_2 + a_3 +... +a_n| \leq |a_1| + |a_2| + ... + |a_n| \\
\end{align}
$$
